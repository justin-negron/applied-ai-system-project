# Common Pet Medication Schedules

These cadences cover the most common preventive medications. Your vet's specific instructions always override these guidelines.

## Heartworm prevention (dogs and cats)

- **Cadence**: monthly, year-round in most climates. Some northern regions allow seasonal dosing (April–November) but year-round is the safer default.
- **Common products**: Heartgard, Sentinel, Trifexis, Revolution.
- **Tip**: pick a memorable day of the month (the 1st, or your pet's "monthiversary") and stick with it. Missing more than 6 weeks between doses requires a heartworm test before resuming.
- **Duration to administer**: 5 minutes (most are flavored chews or topicals).

## Flea and tick prevention

- **Cadence**: monthly for most products (NexGard, Bravecto chew, Frontline). Bravecto chew lasts 3 months.
- **Tip**: same monthly date as heartworm prevention if the products are compatible — many combo products handle both.

## Vaccines (dogs)

- **Core**: rabies (every 1–3 years depending on local law), DHPP/DAPP (distemper, hepatitis, parvo, parainfluenza — every 1–3 years after initial puppy series).
- **Lifestyle-based**: bordetella (kennel cough — annually if boarding, daycare, or grooming), leptospirosis, Lyme.
- **Cadence**: annual wellness visit handles most vaccine boosters.

## Vaccines (cats)

- **Core**: FVRCP (annually after kitten series), rabies (1–3 years).
- **Lifestyle-based**: FeLV (feline leukemia — annually for outdoor or multi-cat households).

## Daily medications (chronic conditions)

- **Insulin** (diabetic pets): typically twice daily, with meals, ~12 hours apart. Consistent timing is critical.
- **Thyroid medication** (cats — methimazole): once or twice daily, often easier as transdermal cream.
- **Joint supplements** (glucosamine, fish oil): once daily with food.
- **Anti-inflammatories** (NSAIDs like Rimadyl, Galliprant): typically once or twice daily with food.

## Tips for medication adherence

- Set phone reminders for daily meds — missed doses for chronic conditions can be serious.
- Use a pill organizer for pets on multiple medications.
- For finicky cats, pill pockets or compounded flavored liquids help.
- Track doses — a simple checkbox calendar prevents double-dosing on busy days.

## When to call the vet

- A missed dose of insulin or seizure medication: call right away.
- A double dose of any medication: call your vet or the ASPCA Poison Control hotline (888-426-4435).
- Vomiting after taking medication: ask whether to re-dose or wait.
