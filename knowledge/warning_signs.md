# Pet Warning Signs and Emergencies

This guide helps you recognize when a symptom is "watch and wait" versus "call the vet now." It is not a substitute for professional veterinary care — when in doubt, call your vet.

## Call the vet immediately (do not wait)

These signs warrant a same-day or emergency vet visit:

- **Difficulty breathing**: open-mouth breathing in cats, blue/pale gums, gasping, persistent coughing.
- **Bloated, hard belly** (especially in deep-chested dogs like Great Danes, Boxers, German Shepherds): signs of GDV/bloat — life-threatening within hours.
- **Unable to urinate or straining**: especially in male cats, this is a urinary blockage and an emergency.
- **Sudden collapse, seizure, or unresponsiveness**.
- **Suspected toxin ingestion**: chocolate, grapes/raisins, xylitol (sugar substitute in gum, peanut butter), lilies (cats), antifreeze, rodenticides, human medications. Call ASPCA Poison Control: 888-426-4435.
- **Bleeding that won't stop after 5 minutes** of direct pressure.
- **Trauma**: hit by car, fall from height, dog fight injury — even if they "seem fine," internal bleeding is possible.
- **Repeated vomiting** (more than 2–3 times in a few hours), especially with lethargy.
- **Bloody diarrhea** with lethargy.
- **Eye injuries**: squinting, swelling, or visible damage.
- **Heatstroke signs**: heavy panting, drooling, vomiting, collapse — cool with damp towels and go to the vet immediately.

## Schedule a vet appointment within 24–48 hours

- Not eating for more than 24 hours (cats: more than 24 hours of not eating risks fatty liver — be quicker to call).
- Persistent diarrhea or vomiting (not bloody, animal still alert).
- Limping that doesn't resolve in a day, or any non-weight-bearing lameness.
- New lump that's growing or painful.
- Excessive thirst and urination (often the first sign of diabetes or kidney issues).
- Bad breath that's notably worse than usual (dental disease, kidney issues).
- Persistent scratching, head-shaking, or licking one spot.
- Behavioral changes: hiding, aggression, sudden lethargy.

## Watch and wait (24 hours)

- Single vomiting or diarrhea episode in an otherwise healthy adult pet.
- Mild limp from over-exertion.
- One-time refusal of food when behavior is otherwise normal.

If symptoms persist or worsen, escalate to a vet appointment.

## Toxic foods (dogs and cats)

| Food | Risk |
|---|---|
| Chocolate (especially dark) | Theobromine toxicity — vomiting, seizures, heart issues |
| Grapes, raisins | Acute kidney failure, even small amounts |
| Onions, garlic, chives, leeks | Damages red blood cells |
| Xylitol (sugar substitute) | Severe hypoglycemia, liver failure — extremely toxic to dogs |
| Macadamia nuts | Weakness, tremors (dogs) |
| Alcohol | Severe toxicity |
| Caffeine | Heart and nervous system damage |
| Cooked bones | Splinter and cause GI obstruction |
| Avocado | Mild GI upset (most pets), heart issues (some species) |

For cats specifically: lilies (all parts of the plant) are extremely toxic — even pollen on fur ingested during grooming.

## How to talk to your vet

When you call, have ready:
- Your pet's species, breed, age, weight
- What you observed and when it started
- Whether they ate anything unusual
- Photos or videos of the symptom (very helpful)

This guide cannot diagnose your pet. PawPal+ does not provide veterinary advice — when symptoms appear, call your vet or an emergency animal hospital.
